My Research

On 14th September 2015, gravitational waves were directly observed for the first time. The discovery by the LIGO collaboration came 100 years after Einstein's prediction in his General Theory of Relativity. Gravitational waves enable the universe to be seen in a new way, by providing information that goes beyond the capabilities of traditional light telescopes.

My research examines how neutron stars, a type of dead star, emit gravitational waves by growing mountains. Neutron stars are made up mostly of neutrons. They are very dense and have incredibly strong gravitational fields. These properties cause neutron stars to be very spherical in their shape. To make gravitational waves, an object(s) must have some form of asymmetry. One way a neutron star can change its shape is by growing mountains. As the deformed neutron star spins, it creates ripples in space and time. It is these ripples that are called gravitational waves. To generate a gravitational wave signal that is detectable on earth, these mountains only need to grow a few millimetres high.

One mechanism that enables mountain growth is the presence of hot spots in the neutron star's crust. Hot spots can arise through a process called accretion. Accretion occurs when the neutron star's gravitational field draws material from a neighbouring star onto its surface. This accreted material is compressed as more accreted material falls on top of it. This compression causes the matter to increase in density. As the density increases, nuclear reactions begin to occur. These reactions generate heat. If the accreted matter falls onto the star unevenly, then hot spots can arise from the heat generated by the nuclear reactions.

The key questions I am trying to answer are: Can hot spots develop in this way? If so, then how long do they live for? Are they hot enough and do they live long enough to generate mountains large enough to produce gravitational wave signals that can be detected here on earth?

Researching the dynamical behaviour of neutron stars provides information on how the laws of physics behave in extreme gravity environments, something that cannot be replicated on earth. Gravitational wave astronomy opens a new window to the universe by illuminating dark matter and many other aspects of our universe waiting to be discovered.
